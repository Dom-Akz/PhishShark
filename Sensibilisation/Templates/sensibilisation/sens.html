What is Phishing?

Phishing is a cyber attack where criminals impersonate trusted organizations to steal passwords, credit card numbers, or other sensitive information. Attacks arrive via email, text message (smishing), or phone calls (vishing).

Attackers create urgency — threatening account closure, promising rewards, or impersonating executives — to trick you into acting without thinking.

How to Spot a Phishing Email

  • Suspicious sender address — Domain misspelled (e.g., "micros0ft.com" instead of "microsoft.com")
  • Urgent or threatening language — "Your account will be closed immediately"
  • Generic greeting — "Dear Customer" or "Valued Member" instead of your name
  • Requests for sensitive information — Legitimate companies never ask for passwords or credit cards by email
  • Suspicious links or attachments — Hover to see real URL; unexpected attachments (ZIP, EXE, DOC macros) are dangerous
  • Poor grammar and spelling — Professional companies proofread their communications

Real Phishing Examples

From: security@accounts-support.com
Subject: ⚠️ Unusual Sign-in Attempt Detected
"Dear customer, someone tried to access your account from an unknown device. Click here to secure your account immediately or it will be permanently locked."
❌ Generic greeting | ❌ Urgent threat | ❌ Suspicious sender domain
From: billing@paypai.com
Subject: Invoice #38491 - Payment Due
"We noticed an unpaid invoice of $1,245.00. Download the attached invoice to review the charges."
❌ Wrong domain ("paypai.com" not "paypal.com") | ❌ Unexpected attachment | ❌ Unfamiliar invoice
From: ceo@company-secure.net
Subject: Urgent - Wire Transfer Needed
"I'm in a meeting and can't talk. Please wire $50,000 to this account immediately. I'll explain later."
❌ Bypasses normal approval | ❌ Urgent request | ❌ Prevents verification ("can't talk")

What To Do If You Receive a Phishing Email

  • STOP — Don't click links, reply, or open attachments
  • HOVER — Hover over links to see the real URL (don't click)
  • VERIFY — Contact the company using official channels (not the email)
  • REPORT — Forward to your IT security team
  • DELETE — Mark as spam and delete the message

If You Already Clicked or Shared Information

  • 1. Disconnect from the internet — Stop malware from spreading
  • 2. Run a security scan — Use your antivirus software immediately
  • 3. Change your passwords — Especially for any accounts using the same password
  • 4. Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) — Add this critical protection to all accounts
  • 5. Contact affected institutions — Call your bank or credit card company if you shared financial info
  • 6. Report to IT — Immediately notify your security team
  • 7. Monitor your accounts — Check for unauthorized activity

How to Protect Yourself

  • Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA/2FA) — The single most effective protection
  • Use a password manager — Generate strong, unique passwords for every account. Never reuse passwords
  • Keep software updated — Enable automatic updates for your OS, browser, and antivirus
  • Back up your data — Use the 3-2-1 rule: 3 copies, 2 media types, 1 offsite
  • Verify before trusting — Always contact senders through official channels you find independently
  • Never share passwords or 2FA codes — Legitimate companies will never ask for them
  • Be skeptical of unexpected messages — Even from coworkers (their accounts may be compromised)
